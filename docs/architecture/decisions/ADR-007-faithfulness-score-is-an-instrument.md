# ADR-007: Faithfulness score is an instrument, not a validity verdict

- Status: Accepted
- Date: 2026-06-21

**In one minute:** we want a number that tells the maintainer whether a change made the notes *more or less faithful* over time — a regression yardstick. We declare it the same species of claim ADR-004 made of the cost figure: an **instrument reading, not a verdict**. It lives outside the §7 mechanical checks and **never gates the pipeline** — `extract.py` and `pipeline.py` must never import `eval`. It is really two numbers we refuse to blend: a deterministic **floor** (a fact, 100% reproducible) and a future stochastic **judge** (a noisy measurement with error bars). A reading only means anything next to its **instrument fingerprint**; change the instrument and you cannot compare across runs. The corpus it runs on is verbatim copyrighted excerpts, so it inherits ADR-003 — private, config-pointed, never committed — which makes the number deliberately **not portable**. And the obvious "LLM grades faithfulness" judge is **deferred**, because the naive form contradicts the methodology's own §4.6 anti-pattern and would Goodhart the notes back toward summarization.

## Context

We keep changing Wave 1 (prompts, effort, the anchor gate) and Wave 2 (dedup, term store, assembly), and we have no answer to the plainest question a maintainer asks: *did this change make the notes more or less faithful to the source?* §7's five mechanical checks tell us a notes-set is *valid* — grounded, traceable, well-typed, referentially whole, schema-valid — but valid is a floor, not a grade. Two valid runs can differ in how much of the source they faithfully captured, and §7 says nothing about which is better.

The temptation is to reach for an LLM that reads each statement against its evidence and emits a faithfulness score, then track that number release to release. That temptation is exactly what this ADR is about — because the obvious version of it is wrong in two distinct ways: it would be a *stochastic measurement reported as if it were a fact*, and it would structurally penalize the very note shapes the methodology treats as first-class (§4.6). We needed to decide what a faithfulness number is *allowed to mean* before building anything that produces one.

This records the design reviewed in the faithfulness-regression-yardstick session. It stands on three accepted decisions: ADR-004 (a tool-produced figure can be non-authoritative by construction), ADR-003 (the copyrighted corpus is private and config-pointed), and the §7.6 frame (software assists, human judges, advisory, never a gate).

## Decision

**The score is an instrument reading, not a validity verdict.** It is the same species of claim ADR-004 made of the cost estimate: a useful figure that no correctness decision depends on. It lives **outside** §7's enforced mechanical checks and **never gates the pipeline** — the eval module is import-isolated, and `extract.py` / `pipeline.py` must never import it (`eval.py`). This is the §7.6 stance — *software assists, the human judges; advisory, never a gate* — applied to the **faithfulness** axis, distinct from §7.6's own *coverage* axis. Coverage asks "was anything important dropped?"; faithfulness asks "is what's here true to the source?" Both are reviewed judgements the software assists but cannot prove.

**Two scores, never blended.** The reading is really two measurements of different kinds, reported side by side and never summed into one headline:

- **The floor** — deterministic. It re-runs the real mechanical facts against the corpus: verbatim-anchoring (§7.2), grounding (§7.1), referential integrity (§7.4), reusing `validation.py` and `normalize.py` rather than re-deriving them. The floor is a **fact: 100% reproducible**, identical bytes in → identical counts out.
- **The judge** — a future stochastic LLM entailment score. It is a **noisy measurement with error bars**, not a fact; the same inputs can yield different numbers across runs.

**The floor is completeness-aware — coverage of the expected pages is part of the floor fact, not just the per-excerpt anchoring of the notes that happen to exist.** Anchoring and grounding are computed only over the notes that *exist*, and that is a trap: a score over present notes alone **silently rewards extraction failure**. We saw it directly — a tuning sweep (effort=high on a dense paper) exhausted `max_tokens` mid-think on 8 of 15 pages and emitted no/truncated JSON; those pages kept **stale notes from the prior run**, and the floor read a *higher, perfect* number (100% anchored, *more* statements) for a run that had actually **lost half the document**. The instrument misled under exactly the conditions a sweep creates. So the floor counts **expected-vs-present pages**, not just per-excerpt anchoring of whatever happens to be there: a corpus doc carries the *expected* note-page set, `eval` reports expected against present and surfaces missing/failed pages, and a **partial or stale-contaminated corpus can never read as a clean 100%**. This is still a fact — a reproducible count of expected vs present pages, identical bytes in → identical counts out — so it sits inside the deterministic floor, not the judge.

Blending a fact and a noisy measurement into a single "faithfulness: 0.87" launders the error bars out of sight. We report them apart, always, and label the judge as the measurement it is.

**A reading is only comparable next to its instrument fingerprint.** A score with no fingerprint is uninterpretable, the way ADR-004's estimate is meaningless without its as-of date. The fingerprint records the corpus id, the timestamp, and the floor counts; and for any future judge, the judge **model id, prompt version, and temperature**. A fingerprint change **invalidates cross-run comparison** — you cannot say a run improved over a prior run measured with a different instrument. This is the same discipline `report.py` applies with its input fingerprint and ADR-004 with its as-of date: a figure carries the conditions that make it mean something.

**Corpus privacy is inherited from ADR-003, not re-decided here.** The eval corpus is verbatim copyrighted excerpts — the most sensitive data the tool handles — so it is **private, config-pointed** (`eval_corpus_dir`), and **never committed**. Only a small **public smoke corpus** (non-copyrighted, self-authored) ships for CI, enough to prove the harness runs. The accepted consequence is that the score is **deliberately not portable**: a third party can run the harness but cannot reproduce *your* number, because they don't have your corpus. That non-portability is the eval analogue of ADR-003's private feedback repo — the same tradeoff, accepted for the same reason.

**The LLM entailment judge is deferred, with rationale — and constrained if ever built.** The naive judge contradicts the methodology's own §4.6 discipline that *faithfulness is never delegated wholesale to one big model call*. A per-(statement, excerpt) entailment score structurally **false-negatives the methodology's first-class shapes**: a statement synthesized from *multiple* evidence records, a statement whose evidence is an attribution footnote, a statement reading a *figure*. None of those is a clean single-pair entailment, so a naive judge marks them unfaithful — and under optimization pressure, maximizing that judge **Goodharts the notes back toward summarization**, the exact failure the tool exists to prevent. So we defer it. If it is ever built, it is a **per-pair triage flagger** — it produces a *list of suspect pairs for a human to confirm*, with the known structural classes (multi-evidence, attribution-footnote, figure-reading) exempt from the flag — and **never an aggregate tracked number**. A flagger assists review; a tracked aggregate becomes an optimization target, and that is the line we will not cross.

**Boundary.** The only inbound arrow is `cli → eval`. Inside, `eval → validation` and `eval → normalize` is **correct and required**: the floor must reuse the *real* checks, not a second copy of them, or it would measure faithfulness against a re-implementation that can drift from what the pipeline actually enforces (COR-007's no-re-implementation discipline). `pipeline → eval` is **forbidden** — it would let a stochastic, corpus-private grade reach into the deterministic extraction path and violate the network-posture isolation (Invariant 5).

## Consequences

- **The floor is shippable now; the judge is not on the roadmap.** Floor-only eval gives the maintainer a real regression signal today — anchoring/grounding/referential counts that move when a change degrades them — with zero stochasticity and zero corpus to grade. The judge stays deferred until someone designs the structural-class exemptions; building the naive version is the failure mode this ADR exists to forbid.
- **A corpus doc must carry its expected-page set, or the floor is blind to loss.** The expected note-page set is captured at corpus-build time, **cli-side via `ingest`** — preserving `eval`'s no-pipeline-import isolation: `eval` reads the manifest the capture *wrote*, it never imports a pipeline module to recompute it. A missing expected page is then a **surfaced gap, never silently dropped from the denominator** — the partial run reports as partial, the stale-contaminated run cannot launder itself into a clean pass.
- **The two numbers must stay visibly separate in any output.** The moment a UI or report sums the floor and the judge into one headline, it has re-introduced the laundering this ADR rejects. Reporting them apart — fact beside measurement — is part of the contract, like ADR-004's "estimate" label.
- **Every reading must carry its fingerprint, or it is noise.** A stored or displayed score without corpus id / timestamp / floor counts (/ judge model+prompt+temp) cannot be compared to anything and should not be trusted as a trend. A future maintainer seeing a score "improve" must check the fingerprint matched first.
- **The eval corpus is a private maintainer asset.** Cross-run comparison is meaningful only for whoever holds the corpus; a contributor without it sees the harness pass on the smoke corpus, not a real faithfulness number. Onboarding a second maintainer means handing over the corpus out of band — the same shape as ADR-003's private repo and out-of-band credential.
- **`eval` must stay import-isolated from the pipeline.** Its whole safety story is that a stochastic, corpus-dependent grade can never gate extraction. A future change that imports `eval` from `extract.py` or `pipeline.py` — even "just to log the score" — breaks Invariant 5 and must be resisted; the reverse arrows (`eval → validation`, `eval → normalize`) are the only coupling allowed.
- **If the judge is ever revived, it returns a list, not a number.** The accepted shape is a triage flagger feeding human review, with structural classes exempt. Anyone proposing an aggregate faithfulness metric to track over time is reopening this ADR and must say so explicitly (the supersession gesture), because that aggregate is precisely the Goodhart target we declined.
